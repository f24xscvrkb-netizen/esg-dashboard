ESG Performance and Corporate Controversies: An Exploratory Analysis

A mini dashboard designed to showcase an analytical ESG approach, combining ESG scoring, controversy proxies, and data limitations.

Normalized view

This chart normalizes controversies by company size (Market Cap) to reduce scale effects. Why it matters: Large firms tend to have higher absolute controversy counts due to scale and visibility. Normalizing by market cap helps compare controversy exposure on a more comparable basis.

Key insights

    Dataset

    The dataset includes a selected sample of companies with ESG scores and publicly reported controversy counts. The data are intended for illustrative purposes, using publicly available ESG-related indicators and simplified proxies rather than proprietary ratings.

    Methodology

    This dashboard aims to provide an intuitive and transparent way to explore the relationship between corporate ESG performance and corporate controversies. Rather than presenting a definitive ranking, the goal is to illustrate how different ESG-related indicators can be jointly interpreted.

    The ESG score used in this dashboard represents a composite measure of a company’s environmental, social, and governance performance, expressed on a standardized scale from 0 to 100. While ESG scores are widely used in practice, they should be understood as relative indicators that depend on the underlying methodology, data sources, and weighting schemes adopted by the provider.

    Corporate controversies are used as a proxy for reputational and operational risk. In this context, controversies refer to publicly reported events related to environmental incidents, social issues, or governance failures. The number of controversies is not a direct measure of misconduct, as it is influenced by factors such as media coverage, disclosure practices, firm size, and geographic exposure.

    By plotting ESG scores against controversy counts, the dashboard highlights potential patterns and trade-offs. Companies located in the bottom-right area of the chart (high ESG scores and relatively few controversies) may be interpreted as exhibiting a more balanced ESG profile, whereas other positions reflect different combinations of performance and risk.

    This dashboard is intended as an exploratory tool rather than a definitive assessment. A professional ESG analysis would require additional controls for firm size, sector-specific dynamics, geographic context, time windows, and severity of controversies, as well as a comparison across multiple ESG rating providers.

    How to read the chart

    • X-axis: ESG Score (higher values indicate better performance according to the selected metric).
    • Y-axis: Number of controversies (higher values indicate more reported events).
    • Quadrants: The ideal position is the bottom-right quadrant (high ESG score, low number of controversies).

    Limitations (important)

    • ESG metrics are not fully standardized; different rating providers may produce different results.
    • Controversy data are influenced by media coverage, disclosure practices, and event definitions.
    • A professional analysis would require controls for firm size, sector, geography, and the chosen time window.
